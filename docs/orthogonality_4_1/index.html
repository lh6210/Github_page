Orthogonality of the Four Subspaces - Chapter 4 (4.1)
📅Published on August 17, 2022 ☕Keywords: Matrix, Sympy
NOTES
LAB
NOTES:
  • Two subspaces $V$ and $W$ of a vector space are orthogonal if every vector $v$ in $V$ is perpendicular to every vector $w$ in $W$: $$ V \perp W \; \textrm{if} \; \mathbf{v}^T \mathbf{w} = \mathbf{0} \; \forall \; \mathbf{v} \in V \; \textrm{and} \; \forall \; \mathbf{w} \in W $$
  • When a vector is in two orthogonal subspaces, it must be zero. Because it has to be perpendicular to itself.
  • The nullspace $N(A)$ and row space $C(A^T)$ are orthogonal subspaces of $R^n$.
    Proof: $$ \textrm{ As we have } \mathbf{x} \in N(A) \textrm{ and } A^T \mathbf{y} \in C(A^T) \textrm{, } (A^T \mathbf{y}) \cdot \mathbf{x} = (A^T \mathbf{y})^T \mathbf{x} =\mathbf{y}^T A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}^T \cdot \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0} $$
  • The orthogonal complement of a subspace $V$ inside a space (let's say space $W$) contains every vector that is perpendicular to $V$ in space $W$. This orthogonal subspace is denoted by $V^{\perp} \subseteq W$.
  • Facts about Orthogonal Complements: Let $W$ be a subspace of $R^n$. Then [1]
    1. $W^{\perp}$ is also a subspace of $R^n$
    2. $(W^{\perp})^{\perp} = W$
    3. $ \textrm{dim}(W) + \textrm{dim}(W^{\perp}) = n $
  • $N(A)^{\perp} = C(A^T) \; \textrm{in} \; \mathcal{R}^{n} : \textrm{dim} (N(A))= n -r; \textrm{dim}(C(A^T)) = r; N(A) \perp C(A^T) \; \textrm{AND} \; \textrm{dim}(N(A)) + \textrm{dim}(C(A^T)) = n $
  • $C(A)^{\perp} = N(A^T) \; \textrm{in} \; \mathcal{R}^{m}$.
  • The point of complements is that every $\mathbf{x}$ can be split into a row space component $\mathbf{x}_r$ and a nullspace component $\mathbf{x}_n$. When $A$ multiple $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}_r + \mathbf{x}_n$, $A \mathbf{x} = A \mathbf{x}_r + A \mathbf{x}_n = A \mathbf{x}_r + \mathbf{0} = A \mathbf{x}_r \in C(A)$.
  • Every vector $\mathbf{b} \in C(A)$ comes from one and only one vector $\mathbf{x}_r \in C(A^T)$.
    Proof:
    Suppose $ \mathbf{b} \in C(A) = A \mathbf{x}_r = A \mathbf{x}_r^{'}$, and $ \mathbf{x}_r, \mathbf{x}_r^{'} \in C(A^T)$. So then $ (\mathbf{x}_r - \mathbf{x}_r^{'}) \in C(A^T)$. Also since $ A (\mathbf{x}_r - \mathbf{x}_r^{'}) = \mathbf{0}$, so then $ (\mathbf{x}_r - \mathbf{x}_r^{'}) \in N(A)$. Therefore, $ \mathbf{x}_r - \mathbf{x}_r^{'}$ have to be $\mathbf{0}$.
  • A row of $A$ can't be in the nullspace of $A$ (except for a zero row).
  • Any $n$ independent vectors in $\mathcal{R}^n$ must span $\mathcal{R}^n$, so they are a basis; any $n$ vectors that span $\mathcal{R}^n$ must be independent.
  • If the $n$ columns of $A$ are independent, they span $\mathcal{R}^n$. So $A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is solvable and has only one solution.
  • Fredholm's Alternative [ex. 7]: Exactly one of these problems has a solution: $$ A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \quad \textrm{OR} \quad A^T \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{0} \; \textrm{with} \; \mathbf{y}^T\mathbf{b} = 1. $$
